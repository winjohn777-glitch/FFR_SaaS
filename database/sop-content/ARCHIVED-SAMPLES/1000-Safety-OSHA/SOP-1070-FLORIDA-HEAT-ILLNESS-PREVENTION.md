# SOP-1070: Florida Heat Illness Prevention and Response

## 1. PURPOSE AND SCOPE

### 1.1 Purpose
This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) establishes comprehensive protocols for preventing heat-related illnesses during roofing operations in Florida's extreme climate conditions. This procedure ensures compliance with OSHA heat illness prevention standards while addressing Florida-specific environmental challenges.

### 1.2 Scope
This SOP applies to all Florida First Roofing LLC operations during:
- Summer months (May through October)
- High heat index days (above 80°F)
- Direct sunlight exposure work
- All roofing and construction activities
- Emergency response situations
- Training and orientation programs

## 2. DEFINITIONS

- **Heat Index**: Measurement combining air temperature and relative humidity to determine perceived temperature
- **Heat Exhaustion**: Serious heat-related illness involving heavy sweating, weakness, and elevated body temperature
- **Heat Stroke**: Life-threatening condition with body temperature above 103°F and altered mental state
- **Acclimatization**: Physiological adaptation to heat exposure over 7-14 days
- **Wet Bulb Globe Temperature (WBGT)**: Heat stress measurement including temperature, humidity, and solar radiation

## 3. RESPONSIBILITIES

- Monitor daily heat index and issue heat alerts
- Develop heat illness prevention training programs

### 3.2 Project Managers

- Authorize work modifications or suspensions
- Communicate heat safety requirements to crews

### 3.3 Field Supervisors
- Conduct pre-shift heat safety briefings
- Monitor individual worker heat stress symptoms
- Enforce mandatory rest and hydration breaks
- Maintain cooling stations and shade structures
- Implement emergency response for heat illness

### 3.4 All Employees
- Follow prescribed hydration schedules
- Recognize personal heat stress symptoms
- Report concerns about coworkers' heat exposure
- Use provided cooling equipment and shade
- Participate in acclimatization programs

## 5. PROCEDURES

### 5.1 Heat Monitoring and Assessment

#### 5.1.1 Daily Heat Index Monitoring
1. **Morning Assessment (6:00 AM)**
   - Check National Weather Service heat index forecast
   - Review WBGT readings from weather monitoring station
   - Assess cloud cover and wind conditions
   - Determine heat alert level for the day

2. **Hourly Updates**
   - Monitor real-time temperature and humidity
   - Track heat index progression throughout day
   - Update crew notifications of changing conditions
   - Adjust work schedules based on heat index

3. **Heat Alert Levels**
   - **Green (Heat Index 80-84°F)**: Normal operations with increased hydration
   - **Yellow (Heat Index 85-89°F)**: Enhanced monitoring and frequent breaks
   - **Orange (Heat Index 90-104°F)**: Modified work schedules and mandatory rest
   - **Red (Heat Index 105°F+)**: Suspension of non-essential outdoor work

#### 5.1.2 Site-Specific Assessment
1. **Surface Temperature Measurement**
   - Use infrared thermometer on roofing surfaces
   - Document temperatures exceeding 140°F
   - Identify hot spots and thermal hazards
   - Plan work routing to minimize exposure

2. **Microclimate Evaluation**
   - Assess wind patterns and air circulation
   - Identify areas with limited shade availability
   - Evaluate heat reflection from surfaces
   - Consider urban heat island effects

### 5.2 Prevention Strategies

#### 5.2.1 Work Schedule Modifications
1. **Early Start Protocols**
   - Begin work at 6:00 AM during high heat periods
   - Complete heat-intensive tasks before 11:00 AM
   - Schedule interior work during peak heat hours
   - Plan administrative tasks for hottest periods

2. **Work/Rest Cycles**
   - **Light Work**: 15-minute break every hour above 85°F heat index
   - **Moderate Work**: 15-minute break every 30 minutes above 90°F
   - **Heavy Work**: 15-minute break every 15 minutes above 90°F
   - **Extreme Conditions**: Mandatory 15-minute break every 15 minutes above 100°F

#### 5.2.2 Hydration Protocols
1. **Pre-Shift Hydration**
   - Consume 16-20 oz of water 2 hours before work
   - Avoid alcohol and excessive caffeine
   - Check urine color for hydration status
   - Document baseline weight for monitoring

2. **During Work Hydration**
   - Consume 6-8 oz of water every 15-20 minutes
   - Use electrolyte replacement drinks for heavy sweating
   - Monitor individual fluid intake with tracking sheets
   - Provide flavored water options to encourage consumption

3. **Hydration Station Setup**
   - Position water stations within 200 feet of work areas
   - Maintain water temperature between 50-60°F
   - Provide individual cups and sanitary dispensing
   - Stock electrolyte replacement supplements

#### 5.2.3 Cooling Measures
1. **Shade Structures**
   - Erect temporary shade canopies at rest areas
   - Utilize existing building shade when possible
   - Position shade structures to follow sun movement
   - Ensure minimum 10 feet x 10 feet coverage per 5 workers

2. **Cooling Equipment**
   - Provide cooling towels and neck wraps
   - Use misting fans in rest areas
   - Maintain air-conditioned vehicles for emergency cooling
   - Supply cooling vests for extreme heat conditions

3. **Personal Cooling Strategies**
   - Wet bandanas and cooling cloths
   - Cold water immersion for wrists and neck
   - Ice packs for break periods
   - Light-colored, loose-fitting clothing

### 5.3 Acclimatization Program

#### 5.3.1 New Employee Acclimatization
1. **Week 1 Schedule**
   - Day 1-2: 50% normal work load, frequent breaks
   - Day 3-4: 60% normal work load, regular monitoring
   - Day 5: 80% normal work load with continued observation

2. **Week 2 Schedule**
   - Day 6-8: 90% normal work load with standard breaks
   - Day 9-10: Full work load with standard heat protocols
   - Ongoing monitoring for first month

#### 5.3.2 Returning Employee Protocol
1. **After Absence Protocol**
   - 1-3 days absent: 75% work load for 2 days
   - 4-7 days absent: 50% work load for 3 days, then gradual increase
   - 8+ days absent: Full acclimatization program

### 5.4 Heat Illness Recognition and Response

#### 5.4.1 Heat Exhaustion Symptoms
1. **Physical Signs**
   - Heavy sweating or stopped sweating
   - Weakness or fatigue
   - Dizziness or lightheadedness
   - Nausea or vomiting
   - Headache
   - Muscle cramps

2. **Behavioral Changes**
   - Confusion or irritability
   - Decreased work performance
   - Coordination problems
   - Unusual behavior or personality changes

#### 5.4.2 Emergency Response Procedures
1. **Heat Exhaustion Response**
   - Move victim to cool, shaded area immediately
   - Remove excess clothing and PPE
   - Apply cool water to skin and use fans
   - Provide cool water if victim is conscious and able to swallow
   - Monitor vital signs and temperature
   - Contact emergency medical services if condition worsens

2. **Heat Stroke Response**
   - Call 911 immediately
   - Begin aggressive cooling measures
   - Apply ice to neck, armpits, and groin
   - Use cold water immersion if available
   - Monitor airway and breathing
   - Continue cooling until emergency services arrive

3. **Documentation Requirements**
   - Complete incident report within 2 hours
   - Record environmental conditions at time of incident
   - Document response actions taken
   - Report to OSHA within 24 hours if hospitalization occurs

### 5.5 Personal Protective Equipment Modifications

#### 5.5.1 Heat-Adapted PPE
1. **Clothing Modifications**
   - Light-colored, loose-fitting work shirts
   - Breathable fabric materials
   - Moisture-wicking undergarments
   - Wide-brimmed hard hats with neck protection

2. **PPE Cooling Enhancements**
   - Cooling inserts for hard hats
   - Ventilated safety harnesses
   - Mesh safety vests when possible
   - Breathable work gloves

#### 5.5.2 PPE Management
1. **Cleaning and Maintenance**
   - Daily cleaning of PPE to remove salt buildup
   - Rotation of PPE to allow drying between uses
   - Regular inspection for heat damage
   - Replacement of compromised equipment
